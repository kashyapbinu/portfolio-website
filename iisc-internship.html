Research Summary

My research focuses on exploring Stochastic Resonance (SR)—a counterintuitive phenomenon where noise, instead of degrading a signal, actually helps amplify and detect weak subthreshold inputs. In this work, I used a transistor-based volatile system as the test platform, analysing how controlled noise influences its nonlinear behaviour.

By simulating circuits in LTSpice and injecting white and pink noise into subthreshold voltage pulses, I examined how the system transitions from almost no response to clear, amplified switching as noise reaches an optimal level. This behaviour was quantified using metrics such as output power, Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR), and covariance, confirming the presence of resonance at specific noise intensities.

A key insight was that not all noise is equal. While zero-mean white noise enhanced signals, it introduced misalignment and false switching. Shifting the mean resolved this, enabling clean pulse detection. Even more promising was pink noise, whose low-frequency structure produced the most stable amplification—especially when constrained to positive values.

These findings highlight how leveraging noise intelligently can reduce energy requirements in sensing systems, making SR a compelling strategy for low-power electronics, neuromorphic computing, and aerospace diagnostics, where weak signals are often difficult to capture in noisy environments.
